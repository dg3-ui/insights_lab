## Event Ledger

### 1997-1998 El Nino Winter — California Flooding and Landslides

| Field | Notes |
|---|---|
| Geography / market | California / Southwest; Southern-tier U.S. wet-pattern analog. |
| What happened | NOAA/NCEI's technical report says the winter of 1997-98 was marked by a record-breaking El Nino and a wetter-than-normal southern third of the U.S.; in California, four weeks of nearly continuous February storminess produced flooding, mudslides, and agriculture disruptions ([NOAA/NCEI 1997-98 El Nino report](https://www.ncei.noaa.gov/monitoring-content/billions/reports/19971201-19980228-severe-storm/tr9802.pdf), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Mechanism illustrated | ENSO can shift seasonal storm odds and moisture pathways, but impacts arrive through individual storms, atmospheric rivers, flooding, clouds, and snowpack dynamics. |
| Magnitude / impact | NOAA/NCEI reported over **$550 million** in California damages, **17** storm-related deaths, and **35** counties declared federal disaster areas during the winter ([NOAA/NCEI 1997-98 El Nino report](https://www.ncei.noaa.gov/monitoring-content/billions/reports/19971201-19980228-severe-storm/tr9802.pdf), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Asset / actor relevance | Solar/hydro/offtaker actors should treat ENSO as a seasonal exposure lens, not a deterministic production forecast. |
| What it licenses | A directional wet-winter / storm-odds historical analog for California/Southwest. |
| What it does NOT license | A current-year plant generation, LMP, or revenue forecast. |

### 2015-2016 Strong El Nino — Drought Relief Was Incomplete

| Field | Notes |
|---|---|
| Geography / market | California drought / hydro context. |
| What happened | EIA says California's strong 2015-16 El Nino brought precipitation and improved snowpack/reservoir conditions, but drought was far from normal; by late April 2016 statewide snowpack water content was **14.5 inches**, **61%** of average, up from **1.1 inches** at the same time in 2015 ([EIA hydropower conditions improve](https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=26332), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Mechanism illustrated | A strong El Nino does not guarantee full drought recovery; precipitation phase, snowpack, antecedent deficits, and reservoir conditions matter. |
| Magnitude / impact | California Energy Commission notes that by end-2015, El Nino modestly improved precipitation but much fell as rain rather than snow, limiting early snowpack accumulation ([CEC 2015 generation](https://www.energy.ca.gov/data-reports/energy-almanac/california-electricity-data/2021-total-system-electric-generation/2015), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Asset / actor relevance | Hydro and solar actors should separate ENSO state from the actual regional water/irradiance state. |
| What it licenses | A caveat: ENSO is a driver/condition, not the output variable. |
| What it does NOT license | A deterministic drought-relief, hydro-generation, or plant-output claim. |

### 2023-2024 Strong El Nino — Wet Pattern, But Weak/Atypical Teleconnection

| Field | Notes |
|---|---|
| Geography / market | California / U.S. southern tier. |
| What happened | NOAA Climate.gov says winter 2023-24 had a strong El Nino, with December-February ONI at **1.8 C**, and precipitation patterns showed similarities to the expected El Nino wet southern-tier pattern, including California atmospheric-river events ([NOAA Climate.gov ENSO post](https://www.climate.gov/news-features/blogs/enso/how-much-did-el-nino-influence-precipitation-over-united-states-past), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Mechanism illustrated | Even strong ENSO events can be modulated by atmospheric rivers, MJO, jet dynamics, and other ocean basins. |
| Magnitude / impact | A 2025 Nature Communications Earth & Environment paper found the 2023/24 El Nino had unusually weak extratropical teleconnections; the PNA teleconnection strength was only **15%–30%** of super El Nino events and less than half of 2009/10 ([Nature CEE paper](https://www.nature.com/articles/s43247-025-02584-8), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Asset / actor relevance | ENSO package confidence should stay low/directional even when the ENSO event is "strong." |
| What it licenses | A confidence cap and non-stationarity / teleconnection-noise caveat. |
| What it does NOT license | Treating ENSO strength as a direct proxy for asset-level output or market impact. |

## Cross-Event Patterns

```text
PATTERN 1  ENSO shifts odds, but atmospheric rivers, jet structure, antecedent drought, and local cloud/snow outcomes decide asset exposure.
PATTERN 2  Strong El Nino does not equal strong local teleconnection.
PATTERN 3  ENSO history supports caveated seasonal monitoring, not exact plant generation or price forecasts.
```

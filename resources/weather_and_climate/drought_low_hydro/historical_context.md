## Event Ledger

### Western U.S. Drought and California Hydro — 2021

| Field | Notes |
|---|---|
| Geography / market | California and Pacific Northwest hydropower regions. |
| What happened | EIA says 2021 historic drought reduced water supply and hydropower generation in the Pacific Northwest and California; California hydropower generation was **48% below** its 2011-2020 average, while Pacific Northwest hydropower was **14% below** its 10-year average ([EIA 2021 drought/hydro article](https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=51839), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Mechanism illustrated | Low snowpack and low reservoir levels reduce hydro energy and flexibility, but plant-level impact depends on reservoir and operations context. |
| Magnitude / impact | EIA says California Sierra Nevada snowpack on April 1, 2021 was **41% below normal**; Shasta hydro generation was **46% lower** than its 10-year average, and Edward Hyatt generation was **81% lower** than its 10-year average after Lake Oroville hit record-low levels ([EIA 2021 drought/hydro article](https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=51839), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Asset / actor relevance | Owners/offtakers/lenders should track basin snowpack and storage, not just plant location. |
| What it licenses | A regional low-hydro exposure claim where water-state sources are current and basin-relevant. |
| What it does NOT license | Exact MWh or revenue loss for another hydro plant without plant-to-basin/reservoir/operations linkage. |

### California Drought and Hydro Mix — 2012-2016 / 2015 Update

| Field | Notes |
|---|---|
| Geography / market | California hydro fleet. |
| What happened | California Energy Commission data says 2015 hydroelectric generation was at the lowest level of the prior 15 years, dropping **15%** from 2014 and **67%** since 2011, the last wet year in California ([CEC 2015 generation](https://www.energy.ca.gov/data-reports/energy-almanac/california-electricity-data/2021-total-system-electric-generation/2015), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Mechanism illustrated | Strong precipitation events or El Niño can improve water conditions without fully restoring depleted snowpack/reservoir systems. |
| Magnitude / impact | Pacific Institute reported that reduced hydroelectric production during the four water years ending September 30, 2015 added approximately **$2.0 billion** in economic cost to California ratepayers and that hydropower provided less than **7%** of total in-state electricity in water year 2015 ([Pacific Institute 2015 update](https://pacinst.org/publication/impacts-of-californias-ongoing-drought-hydroelectricity-generation-2015-update/), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Asset / actor relevance | Investors/lenders should treat sustained drought as a multi-year portfolio / contract-shape exposure. |
| What it licenses | A multi-year drought materiality frame. |
| What it does NOT license | A current plant-level generation forecast or PPA impact. |

### Colorado River / Glen Canyon and Hoover — 2022-2023

| Field | Notes |
|---|---|
| Geography / market | Colorado River / Southwest hydro. |
| What happened | EIA says Western U.S. hydropower generation in the 2022-23 water year fell to the lowest level since at least 2001, dropping **11%** from the prior water year to **141.6 million MWh** ([EIA Western hydro 22-year low](https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=61645), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Mechanism illustrated | Reservoir elevation and releases matter as much as drought category; low reservoir levels can reduce head and turbine efficiency. |
| Magnitude / impact | A Congressional Research Service report says each additional 1-foot decline at Lake Powell equates to a loss of **3 MW** of generating capacity at Glen Canyon Dam, and that the plant averaged **4,600 GWh** annually before the current drought compared with **3,800 GWh** from 2000 to 2020 ([CRS Glen Canyon report R47497](https://www.everycrsreport.com/reports/R47497.html), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Asset / actor relevance | Hydro offtakers and WAPA/customers face replacement-power and cost exposure when reservoir hydropower declines. |
| What it licenses | A reservoir-head / power-pool mechanism claim. |
| What it does NOT license | Transferable MWh loss for non-Colorado River plants. |

## Cross-Event Patterns

```text
PATTERN 1  Hydro impact is basin/reservoir-specific, not merely state-specific.
PATTERN 2  Snowpack timing and reservoir storage mediate generation; monthly generation alone does not prove drought causality.
PATTERN 3  Low hydro often shifts system reliance to gas/imports/replacement power, but exact $ impact is source-specific.
```

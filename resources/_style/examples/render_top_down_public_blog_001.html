A ~96% Chance of El Niño Meets 36.8 GW of California Solar

NOAA now puts El Niño at roughly 96% for this winter. For California's utility-scale solar fleet, that tilts an already weak season lower.

The takeaway
  • NOAA CPC carries an El Niño Watch with a ~96% chance of El Niño for winter 2026-27 (DJF). Event strength is still unknown.
  • El Niño winters tend wetter and cloudier across California, which works against solar output in its weakest season.
  • The exposed set is large: ~36.8 GW of California solar, including Topaz, Desert Sunlight, and the Darden complex.
  • This is a directional read. No dollar, price, or production-loss figure survives the evidence we have today.

CONFIDENCE: LOW

What changed

As of June 5, NOAA's Climate Prediction Center holds an El Niño Watch: a ~96% chance that El Niño conditions are present during December through February, with Niño-3.4 already at +0.4 °C. One thing the forecast does not yet say is how strong the event gets. No strength category clears 37%, and strength is what sets the size of any winter effect.

The mechanism, in one breath

El Niño strengthens the subtropical jet. That steers wetter, cloudier winters into California and the Southwest. Less winter sun means a lower winter capacity factor, and winter is already the fleet's low season. Desert Sunlight 300 runs near 0.38 in summer and near 0.13 in winter; an El Niño winter pushes on the low number, not the high one.

What we are not claiming

No $/MWh or LMP effect. No plant-level production forecast. The CA winter teleconnection is moderate but probabilistic, and a single winter can defy it: 2022-23 was a wet La Niña winter, the opposite of the textbook expectation. Effect size scales with event strength, which is not yet known. Revenue outcomes depend on demand, hydro, congestion, and contract structure besides weather.

What to watch

Strength. The forecast sharpens over summer and fall, and the exposure read sharpens with it. Owners with concentrated California solar books (Clearway, BHE, Intersect, Terra-Gen, Avantus) should watch winter generation variance. PG&E, holding 28 contracts on Desert Sunlight 300 alone, has volume and settlement sensitivity on linked supply. Lenders can treat this as a caveated downside scenario for a weak-generation winter, nothing more.

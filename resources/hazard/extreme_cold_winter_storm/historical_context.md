# Historical Context — Extreme Cold / Winter Storm Exposure

> **Status**: draft 2026-06-25.
>
> **Role**: `frames` + `external` evidence. This file informs the mechanism, materiality band, confidence cap, caveats, and blocked claims for `extreme_cold_winter_storm`. It **never** grounds scoped assets in a new applied insight; re-ground every event, number, and entity live before use.

## How To Use This File

```text

          and which claim boundaries it teaches
```

The event ledger is deliberately selective. It favors authoritative system reports (FERC/NERC, ERCOT, PJM, EIA, Texas DSHS) over news summaries. If an event has no strong primary source for a figure, the figure is omitted.

## Event Ledger

### February 2011 Southwest Cold Weather Event — February 1-5, 2011

| Field | Notes |
|---|---|
| Geography / market | Southwest U.S.; Texas, New Mexico, and Arizona were central to the FERC/NERC inquiry. |
| What happened | FERC and NERC released an August 2011 report on outages and curtailments during the February 1-5, 2011 Southwest cold weather event; the FERC/NERC report says about **1.3 million electric customers lacked service at the peak on February 2**, **4.4 million electric customers were affected over February 2-4**, and local distribution companies interrupted gas service to **more than 50,000 gas customers** in New Mexico, Arizona, and Texas ([FERC/NERC 2011 report PDF](https://www.nerc.com/globalassets/our-work/reports/event-reports/sw_cold_weather_event_final.pdf), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Mechanism illustrated | The same coupled mechanism later seen in Uri: cold-weather failures in generation + gas delivery, with gas curtailments lasting longer because relighting customers had to be done manually ([FERC/NERC 2011 report PDF](https://www.nerc.com/globalassets/our-work/reports/event-reports/sw_cold_weather_event_final.pdf), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Magnitude / impact | The full FERC/NERC report is the authoritative source for the detailed outage and curtailment causes behind these customer-impact figures ([FERC/NERC 2011 report PDF](https://www.nerc.com/globalassets/our-work/reports/event-reports/sw_cold_weather_event_final.pdf), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Asset / actor relevance | Owners and lenders should treat this as evidence that Southern-latitude winterization was a known pre-Uri failure mode, not a one-off 2021 surprise. |
| What it licenses | A recurrence / prior-warning claim: cold-weather preparation, fuel assurance, and gas-electric coordination had already failed at meaningful scale before Uri. |
| What it does NOT license | It does **not** prove that any specific modern plant is under-winterized, nor that the 2011 recommendations would have prevented Uri at a named facility. |

### Winter Storm Uri — February 8-20, 2021

| Field | Notes |
|---|---|
| Geography / market | Texas and the South Central U.S.; ERCOT, SPP, and MISO South were affected. |
| What happened | The FERC/NERC final report describes the February 8-20, 2021 event and says **1,045 individual BES generating units** experienced outages, derates, or failures to start; a FERC summary says ERCOT ordered about **20,000 MW** of rolling blackouts, more than **4.5 million people** in Texas lost power, and the event was the largest manually controlled load-shedding event in U.S. history ([FERC/NERC 2021 report PDF](https://www.nerc.com/globalassets/our-work/reports/event-reports/february_2021_cold_weather_report.pdf), [FERC final-report summary](https://ferc.gov/news-events/news/final-report-february-2021-freeze-underscores-winterization-recommendations), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Mechanism illustrated | FERC says freezing issues (**44.2%**) and fuel issues (**31.4%**) caused **75.6%** of unplanned generating-unit outages, derates, and failures to start; it also says natural gas-fired units represented **58%** of generating units with unplanned outages/derates/failures to start, with wind at **27%**, coal at **6%**, solar at **2%**, other at **7%**, and nuclear below **1%** ([FERC final-report summary](https://ferc.gov/news-events/news/final-report-february-2021-freeze-underscores-winterization-recommendations), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Magnitude / impact | A FERC/NERC preliminary presentation says the total Event firm load shed exceeded **23,400 MW**, with ERCOT at about **20,000 MW** at its worst point, SPP at about **2,700 MW**, and MISO South at about **700 MW** ([FERC/NERC preliminary findings presentation](https://www.ferc.gov/sites/default/files/2021-09/2021%20Cold%20Weather%20Inquiry%20Open%20Meeting%20Presentation%20_PPT%20Version.pdf), accessed 2026-06-25). Texas DSHS confirmed **246 winter-storm-related deaths** as of October 27, 2021, across 77 counties ([Texas DSHS mortality report](https://www.dshs.texas.gov/sites/default/files/news/updates/SMOC_FebWinterStorm_MortalitySurvReport_12-30-21.pdf), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Asset / actor relevance | Owners: cold-weather packages, heat tracing, instrumentation protection, fuel assurance. Investors/lenders: correlated outage / DSCR stress where a portfolio concentrates in Southern-latitude gas and wind. Developers: winterization spec is an underwriting input, not an afterthought. |
| What it licenses | A strong regional mechanism claim: extreme cold can simultaneously hit gas supply, gas-fired generation, wind icing, and customer load; freezing and fuel issues are not secondary footnotes. |
| What it does NOT license | It does **not** license a plant-level outage claim from monthly CF, a plant-specific winterization-status claim, an exact MWh/$ loss for a named plant, or a stationary return-period claim. |

### Winter Storm Elliott — December 21-26, 2022

| Field | Notes |
|---|---|
| Geography / market | U.S. Eastern Interconnection; PJM was a major affected market, alongside other Eastern entities. |
| What happened | The FERC/NERC Elliott report says **1,702 individual BES generating units** in the Eastern Interconnection experienced **3,565** unplanned outages, derates, or failures to start; at the worst point, **90,500 MW** of coincident unplanned generating-unit outages/derates/failures to start occurred, and over **127,000 MW** of generation was unavailable including already-out-of-service generation ([FERC/NERC Winter Storm Elliott report](https://www.ferc.gov/sites/default/files/2024-02/24_Winter-Storm_Elliot_0207_UPDATE.pdf), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Mechanism illustrated | The FERC/NERC report says, over the event area, the affected units were **47% natural gas-fired**, **21% wind**, **12% coal**, **3% solar**, **0.4% nuclear**, and **17% other**; it attributes **96%** of outages/derates/failures to start to freezing issues, fuel issues, and mechanical/electrical issues, with **55%** caused by freezing or fuel issues ([FERC/NERC Winter Storm Elliott report](https://www.ferc.gov/sites/default/files/2024-02/24_Winter-Storm_Elliot_0207_UPDATE.pdf), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Magnitude / impact | PJM's Elliott report says almost **47,000 MW** of PJM generation capacity was on forced outage at one point and **92%** of generator outages gave PJM operators one hour of notice or less; PJM also states gas generators accounted for **70%** of outages on December 24 ([PJM Winter Storm Elliott report](https://www.pjm.com/-/media/DotCom/library/reports-notices/special-reports/2023/20230717-winter-storm-elliott-event-analysis-and-recommendation-report.pdf), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Asset / actor relevance | This is the strongest East/PJM analog for gas-fuel and cold-performance risk. It shows the resource is not an ERCOT-only story: gas availability, cold-start readiness, and outage notice quality matter across winter-peaking markets. |
| What it licenses | A cross-market recurrence claim: the same gas-electric / freezing / mechanical failure family appeared in the Eastern Interconnection after Uri, despite different market design and geography. |
| What it does NOT license | It does **not** license a Texas-specific winterization conclusion for PJM plants, or a plant-level outage claim unless a unit-level source is named. |

### January 2025 Arctic Events — January 2025

| Field | Notes |
|---|---|
| Geography / market | Majority of Eastern and Texas Interconnections; the FERC/NERC review covered Winter Storms Blair, Cora, Demi, and Enzo. |
| What happened | The FERC/NERC January 2025 system performance review says natural gas demand peaked at **150 Bcf/day**, electric demand peaked at **683 GW**, and unplanned generator outages peaked at **71,022 MW** between January 21 and 22, 2025, while manual load shed was not required ([FERC/NERC January 2025 review PDF](https://www.nerc.com/globalassets/programs/event-analysis/25_arctic_storms_performance_review_0416.pdf), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Mechanism illustrated | The same stress axis was present (extreme cold + high gas and power demand), but FERC/NERC says improved generator preparedness, proactive commitments, improved situational awareness, and better gas-electric coordination helped the system operate without manual load shed ([FERC January 2025 report announcement](https://www.ferc.gov/news-events/news/ferc-nerc-issue-report-system-performance-during-january-2025-arctic-weather), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Magnitude / impact | FERC/NERC directly compares prior events: Uri had **61,305 MW** of unavailable generation and **23,418 MW** firm load shed; Elliott had **90,500 MW** unavailable generation and **5,400 MW** firm load shed; January 2025 had **71,022 MW** unavailable generation and **no** firm load shed ([FERC/NERC January 2025 review PDF](https://www.nerc.com/globalassets/programs/event-analysis/25_arctic_storms_performance_review_0416.pdf), accessed 2026-06-25). EIA says the week ending January 24, 2025 saw a **321 Bcf** Lower-48 storage withdrawal, nearly **70%** above the 2020-24 five-year average withdrawal for that week ([EIA Today in Energy, Feb. 2025](https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=64524), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Asset / actor relevance | This is a positive-control event: high outage MW did not automatically become load shed. Owners and lenders should distinguish **hazard stress** from **system consequence**, and ask what preparedness and fuel strategy changed. |
| What it licenses | A qualified improvement claim: post-Uri/Elliott preparedness and coordination can reduce consequences, but the system still saw very large unplanned outages. |
| What it does NOT license | It does **not** license "the grid is fixed," nor does it erase plant-specific winterization/fuel-supply diligence. |

## Cross-Event Patterns

```text
PATTERN 1  Gas-electric coupling is the recurring mechanism. 2011, Uri, and Elliott all show that generator
           availability and upstream gas deliverability are one coupled system under cold stress.

PATTERN 2  Cold weather failures are not only "lack of fuel." Frozen instrumentation, controls, valves,
           sensing lines, wind turbine blade icing, snow/ice, mechanical/electrical failures, and fuel pressure
           all show up as separate failure modes. A resource claim must name the mechanism.

PATTERN 3  Monthly generation cannot identify the event. Uri and Elliott happened over days, while the substrate
           generation data is monthly. The event ledger strengthens the mechanism, not plant-level attribution.

PATTERN 4  Preparedness changes outcomes. January 2025 shows large outage MW can be managed without manual load
           shed when commitments, situational awareness, and gas-electric coordination improve — but this is a
           consequence claim, not a plant-proof claim.

PATTERN 5  Non-stationarity and recurrence caution. Repeated cold-weather events do not create a stationary
           return-period; they show the value of winterization and fuel-security diligence under a shifting climate
           and changing generation/gas system.
```

## Event Ledger

### Hurricane Beryl — Texas Gulf Coast, July 8, 2024

| Field | Notes |
|---|---|
| Geography / market | Matagorda / Southeast Texas; ERCOT coastal wind and broader grid infrastructure corridor. |
| What happened | NHC Advisory 39 said Beryl made landfall near Matagorda, Texas at 4:00 AM CDT on July 8, 2024, with maximum sustained winds near 80 mph and dangerous storm surge / flash flooding expected ([NHC advisory](https://www.nhc.noaa.gov/archive/2024/al02/al022024.public.039.shtml), accessed 2026-06-25). NOAA Houston/Galveston also states Beryl made landfall near Matagorda at 4 AM as a Category 1 hurricane with peak over-water sustained winds of 80 mph ([NWS Houston/Galveston Beryl page](https://www.weather.gov/hgx/beryl2024), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Mechanism illustrated | For onshore wind, a Category 1 landfall primarily supports a temporary cut-out / grid-outage frame, not a structural turbine-damage claim. |
| Magnitude / impact | Texas Tribune reporting says Beryl knocked out power for millions of Texans and Matagorda County was hardest hit, but it does not establish wind-turbine damage or plant-level generation impact ([Texas Tribune](https://www.texastribune.org/2024/07/08/hurricane-beryl-texas-damage-updates-rain/), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Asset / actor relevance | Coastal wind owners and lenders should distinguish storm passage, grid outage, and turbine structural damage. |
| What it licenses | A regional storm-near-asset context and cut-out-vs-damage teaching case. |
| What it does NOT license | A Beryl-caused wind plant CF dip, exact wind-farm outage duration, turbine damage, or $ loss. |

### Hurricane Harvey — Texas Gulf Coast, August 25-30, 2017

| Field | Notes |
|---|---|
| Geography / market | ERCOT Gulf Coast; Corpus Christi, Houston/Galveston, Beaumont/Port Arthur. |
| What happened | EIA says Harvey caused more than 10,000 MW of ERCOT generation forced outages and hundreds of transmission/distribution line outages, with power plant outages caused largely by rain/flooding affecting fuel supplies, transmission outages connecting generators to the grid, and personnel access limitations ([EIA Harvey article](https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=32892), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Mechanism illustrated | Harvey is a useful multi-mechanism contrast: wind turbines may cut out in high winds, but much grid impact came through flooding, transmission, fuel, and access. |
| Magnitude / impact | NERC's event analysis says Harvey impacted about 225 transmission assets, rendered a maximum of 10,992 MW of generation capacity unavailable, and caused over 1.67 million reported customer outages in ERCOT ([NERC Harvey event analysis](https://www.nerc.com/globalassets/our-work/reports/event-reports/nerc_hurricane_harvey_ear_20180309.pdf), accessed 2026-06-25). |
| Asset / actor relevance | Wind investors should avoid treating storm impacts as only turbine wind loading; interconnection and access can bind. |
| What it licenses | A broader hurricane-operability caveat for coastal wind: storm impact can land on grid/fuel/access before turbine damage. |
| What it does NOT license | A wind-specific structural damage claim without turbine-level damage records. |

## Cross-Event Patterns

```text
PATTERN 1  Category and mechanism matter. Cat 1–2 landfalls mostly teach cut-out / grid-outage discipline for modern
           onshore wind; structural damage needs stronger evidence.
PATTERN 2  Monthly CF is too coarse for multi-day storm cut-out; summer wind lulls and curtailment confound attribution.
PATTERN 3  Coastal hurricane events often hit transmission, substations, access, and load before they prove turbine damage.
```
